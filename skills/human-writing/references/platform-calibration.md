# Platform Calibration

These are optional editorial heuristics, not claims about platform algorithms or current rules. Load this file only for an explicit platform-adaptation request.

Keep compliance and editorial precedence separate.

Compliance order:

1. fact integrity and safety
2. current mandatory constraints verified from an authoritative source
3. supplied authoritative publication or community rules
4. required revision, relationship, and creation-process disclosures

Editorial order:

1. the user's purpose and presentation preferences
2. the supplied brief
3. the selected genre profile
4. the profiles below as non-normative heuristics

A user may request a deliberately noncompliant format, but do not label that artifact compliant or platform-ready. A user preference may override a heuristic, never a verified mandatory constraint.

If current verification is unavailable, do not present a static profile as a current platform requirement and do not block ordinary adaptation unless a material hard constraint is unknown. Preserve source meaning and label unverifiable recommendations as editorial assumptions only when commentary is requested.

For every platform, preserve material-interest disclosures, source attribution, claim scope, and any current platform-required creation-process disclosure. Platform adaptation may change wording or placement, but it must not make interested promotion look independent, turn documented or source-reported behavior into personal testing, broaden a precise technical claim, or conceal how the artifact was produced when disclosure is required.

Preserve source visibility as well as source accuracy. Background Git history, chats, logs, and editorial research do not become platform copy merely because they informed the adaptation; required attribution and disclosures remain visible.

## Personal Blog

Reader expectation:

- an identifiable author
- durable context
- reasoning and tradeoffs
- enough detail to revisit later

Use:

- first person when genuine
- project history and decisions
- unresolved constraints
- links, code, and evidence
- stable source links and dates for changeable claims
- source-interest labels when a vendor, employer, sponsor, or product owner reports a result
- exact technical subjects, measured operations, and baselines when summarizing external sources
- an ending that records the current judgment

Avoid:

- search-engine filler
- public-account uplift
- release-note structure
- removing every personal edge
- repeating a first-party benchmark as an independent conclusion

## Zhihu-Style Answer

Reader expectation:

- the question answered early
- a defensible position
- explanation, evidence, and exceptions

Use:

- a direct first paragraph
- clear scope: `在我的项目里`, `如果前提是...`
- counterexamples or conditions
- explicit attribution when a key premise comes from an interested source
- section headings only when the answer is long

Avoid:

- spending several paragraphs restating the question
- fake authority
- universal conclusions from one project
- `谢邀`, `以上`, or rhetorical habits added mechanically

## Juejin / CSDN-Style Technical Post

Reader expectation:

- a problem that can be recognized
- a solution that can be reproduced
- code, environment, and verification

Use:

- tested versions when known
- commands close to their explanation
- expected output or verification evidence
- labels for personally tested, documented-only, source-reported, independently reproduced, and inferred behavior
- exact component names and metric scope, including the measured operation, baseline, sample, maturity status, and source relationship
- common errors and boundaries
- a concise title with the technology and task

Avoid:

- generic industry background
- code dumps without a decision chain
- claiming success without a validation step
- hiding outdated or unverified versions
- turning a benchmark for one operation into a claim about the entire language, framework, or product
- converting a vendor benchmark into narrator voice without attribution

## WeChat Public Account / General Chinese Long-Form

Reader expectation:

- an accessible opening
- readable paragraph chunks
- a clear narrative or argument
- enough context for non-specialists

Use:

- one concrete scene or tension near the opening
- short paragraphs around dense points
- selective explanation of terms without replacing precise technical nouns with inaccurate general labels
- visible attribution for first-party claims, including in the title or summary when the claim leads the article
- a restrained conclusion or action

Avoid:

- suspense that delays the real point
- sensational titles unsupported by the body
- headlines that remove `官方称`, test scope, or other qualifiers preserved in the body
- moral uplift and forced emotional peaks
- aggressive calls to follow, share, or buy unless explicitly requested

## SSPAI-Style Product or Experience Article

Reader expectation:

- actual use context
- concrete workflow
- personal preference with reasons
- strengths and limitations
- transparent interest and sourcing

Use:

- what the author was trying to solve
- how the tool fits into a real workflow
- screenshots or operational detail when available
- who benefits and who may not
- a plain self-promotion, employment, sponsorship, or supplied-access disclosure when relevant
- primary sources for specifications, release facts, and documented behavior
- a clear distinction among vendor-reported claims, personally tested results, and independent evidence
- named devices, codecs, versions, settings, workloads, or conditions when they materially affect an experience claim
- useful development insight, workflow knowledge, or decision criteria beyond the product description
- current official creation and disclosure rules immediately before publication; record the authoritative source and verification date when compliance matters
- only actual process details. Do not invent a model, prompt, human-review step, correction, or usage extent to satisfy the rule

Avoid:

- feature-list advertising
- pretending first impressions are long-term evidence
- treating the product maker's benchmark as independent validation
- hiding cost, friction, alternatives, or material relationships
- synthetic lifestyle scenes
- omitting required AI-assistance disclosure or describing substantial assistance as `仅检查错别字`

## X / Weibo / Short Social Post

Reader expectation:

- one point per post
- immediate context
- a detail worth reacting to

Use:

- a direct first sentence
- one contrast or observation
- a natural final line
- a plain ownership or sponsorship disclosure when the post recommends an interested product
- a link only when the post remains understandable without it
- exact technical nouns, metric qualifiers, and source attribution even when space is tight

Avoid:

- article outlines
- hashtag piles
- chained slogans
- fake controversy or engagement bait
- merging different source claims into one broader sentence to save characters
- dropping `官方称` or `厂商测试` while keeping the promotional number

## Reddit / Hacker News

Reader expectation:

- transparent self-interest
- concrete build details
- honest limitations
- no launch theater

Use:

- plain title
- what was built or learned
- the tradeoff
- explicit vendor, employer, or sponsor attribution for reported results
- a precise question only when feedback is genuinely needed

Avoid:

- `game changer`
- fake humility
- link-first promotion
- vague `would love feedback`
- presenting first-party results as community consensus

Keep the title plain, disclose self-interest early, include concrete build details and limitations, and end with a precise question only when feedback is genuinely needed. Do not add launch theater, fake humility, link-first promotion, community-consensus claims, or vague `would love feedback` language.

## Medium / DEV Community

Reader expectation:

- a readable story or practical tutorial
- scannable sections
- enough implementation detail to justify the length

Use:

- a concrete opening
- meaningful headings
- examples and takeaways tied to the body
- transparent authorship, material interests, source interests, and sourcing
- precise terminology and benchmark scope when translating or adapting technical claims
- attribution that survives title, deck, summary, and social excerpt

Avoid:

- generic thought-leadership framing
- SEO-shaped repetition
- a summary that merely restates every heading
- copying a Chinese platform structure word for word
- broadening a first-party claim during translation or headline compression
